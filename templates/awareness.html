Start here — the essential knowledge every internet user should have.

What is Cybersecurity?

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting computers, networks, and data from digital attacks, unauthorized access, and damage. It covers everything from your personal phone to national infrastructure.

Key Point: Everyone is a potential target — not just large companies.
track_changes

The CIA Triad

The three core principles of information security:

  • Confidentiality — Data is only accessible to authorized people
  • Integrity — Data is accurate and has not been tampered with
  • Availability — Systems are accessible when needed
lock

Defense in Depth

Don't rely on a single security measure. Use multiple layers of protection:

  • Strong passwords + MFA
  • Antivirus + Firewall
  • Regular updates + Backups
  • User awareness + Monitoring
bolt

The Human Factor

Over 90% of cyber attacks involve human error. Attackers exploit psychology — urgency, fear, greed, and trust — to bypass technical defenses.

Golden Rule: Always pause and think before clicking, downloading, or sharing information.
Authentication

keyPassword Security

Your password is often the only thing standing between an attacker and your account.

verified_user

Strong Password Formula

  • Minimum 12 characters (16+ preferred)
  • Mix of UPPERCASE and lowercase letters
  • Include numbers (0–9)
  • Include symbols (!@#$%^&*)
  • No dictionary words or predictable patterns
  • Never reuse passwords across accounts
folder_open

Use a Password Manager

A password manager generates, stores, and autofills strong unique passwords for every account. You only need to remember one master password.

Recommended:

  • Bitwarden (free, open-source)
  • 1Password (premium)
  • KeePassXC (offline, local)
smartphone

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

MFA adds a second layer of verification beyond your password. Even if your password is stolen, attackers can't log in without the second factor.

MFA Types (strongest → weakest):

  • 🔑 Hardware key (YubiKey)
  • 📲 Authenticator app (Authy, Google Auth)
  • 📧 Email code
  • 📞 SMS code (least secure)

Social Engineering

phishingRecognizing Phishing

Phishing is the #1 attack vector. Learn to spot and report it every time.

mail

Email Red Flags

  • flagSender address doesn't match display name
  • flagUrgent or threatening subject lines
  • flag"Your account has been suspended"
  • flag"You've won a prize!"
  • flagRequests for passwords or payment
  • flagUnexpected attachments (.exe, .zip)
  • flagPoor grammar and spelling
  • flagGeneric greetings ("Dear Customer")
link

Spotting Fake URLs

  • paypal.com vs paypa1.com
  • amazon.com vs amazon-secure.net
  • Subdomains: paypal.evil.com
  • HTTP (no padlock) on login pages
  • Shortened URLs hiding destinations
  • Extra dashes: micro-soft.com
Tip: Hover over any link to see its true destination before clicking.
smartphone

SMS Phishing (Smishing)

Attackers send fake SMS messages pretending to be banks, delivery services, or government agencies.

  • Never click links in unexpected texts
  • Call the company directly to verify
  • Banks never ask for PINs via text
  • Delivery companies use official apps
phone

Voice Phishing (Vishing)

Attackers call you impersonating tech support, banks, or government offices to steal information.

  • Microsoft never calls you unsolicited
  • IRS communicates by mail, not phone
  • Never give remote access to strangers
  • Hang up and call the official number
Infrastructure

wifi_lockNetwork Security

Secure your connection at home, at work, and on the go.

home

Home Network Security

  • Use WPA3 or WPA2 encryption
  • Change default router admin credentials
  • Create a separate guest WiFi network
  • Keep router firmware updated
  • Disable WPS (known vulnerabilities)
  • Review connected devices regularly
coffee

Public WiFi Risks

Public WiFi at cafés, airports, and hotels is a common attack vector for MitM attacks.

  • check_circleAlways use a VPN on public WiFi
  • check_circleStick to HTTPS sites only
  • cancelNever access banking on public WiFi
  • cancelDon't auto-connect to unknown networks
lock

VPN — Virtual Private Network

A VPN encrypts all traffic between your device and the VPN server, hiding your activity from eavesdroppers.

  • Essential on public WiFi
  • Hides your IP address
  • Choose providers with no-log policies
  • Recommended: ProtonVPN, Mullvad
local_fire_department

Firewall Basics

A firewall monitors and controls incoming/outgoing network traffic based on security rules.

  • Always keep OS firewall enabled
  • Configure application-level rules
  • Block unused ports and services
  • Review firewall logs for anomalies
Devices

smartphoneMobile Security

Your smartphone holds more personal data than any other device — protect it accordingly.

install_mobile

App Security

  • Only install apps from official stores
  • Check app permissions before installing
  • Remove apps you no longer use
  • Keep apps updated to patch vulnerabilities
  • Be suspicious of apps asking excessive permissions
lock_open

Device Locking

  • Use strong PIN (6+ digits) or biometrics
  • Enable auto-lock after 30 seconds
  • Enable full-device encryption
  • Enable remote wipe capability
  • Never use pattern unlock (easily observed)
cloud

Mobile Data & Backup

  • Enable encrypted cloud backup
  • Review which apps sync to cloud
  • Use Signal for sensitive messaging
  • Be careful with location sharing
  • Check location permissions per app
report

If Your Phone is Lost/Stolen

  • Use "Find My Device" to locate/wipe
  • Change passwords for all accounts
  • Contact bank to freeze mobile banking
  • Revoke sessions on all accounts
  • Report to police for insurance purposes
Expert Level

scienceAdvanced Security Practices

Take your security posture to the next level with these advanced techniques.

search

Principle of Least Privilege

Grant users and systems only the minimum permissions necessary to perform their tasks.

  • Use standard user accounts daily (not admin)
  • Remove unused user accounts
  • Audit permissions quarterly
  • Use role-based access control (RBAC)
folder_copy

Backup Strategy (3-2-1 Rule)

  • 3 copies of your data
  • 2 different storage media
  • 1 offsite/cloud location
  • Test backups regularly (restore drills)
  • Encrypt all backup data
bar_chart

Security Monitoring

  • Monitor login activity on all accounts
  • Set up breach alerts (HaveIBeenPwned)
  • Review system event logs weekly
  • Use intrusion detection systems (IDS)
  • Monitor outbound network connections
science

Security Testing

  • Run vulnerability scans on your network
  • Conduct phishing simulation tests
  • Perform regular penetration testing
  • Test your incident response plan
  • Use tools: Nmap, Wireshark, Metasploit

crisis_alertReal-World Breaches & Scams

Learn from real incidents. These attacks affected millions — know what to watch for.

Critical
Ransomware Kenya

President's Website Hacked — Bitcoin Ransom

calendar_today July 18, 2026 corporate_fare State House Kenya

Hackers breached president.go.ke, defaced the homepage with messages targeting President Ruto, and demanded 5 Bitcoin (~KSh 41.3M) ransom — the second major government website attack in under a year.

Attack vector: Website defacement. Attackers replaced the official homepage with ransom demands, a cryptocurrency wallet, and threats to leak unspecified data. ICT CS William Kabogo confirmed the incident, and the site was taken offline for forensic analysis.

Timeline: The defacement was detected at approximately 3:00 AM EAT on July 18, 2026. Visitors to president.go.ke were greeted with a black page displaying ransom demands in English and Kiswahili, a Bitcoin wallet address (bc1q...8k3m), and a 72-hour countdown timer. The ICT Authority was notified within 30 minutes and took the site offline by 4:15 AM. A forensic audit by KE-CIRT confirmed no downstream compromise of internal State House systems.

Attacker profile: A hacktivist group claiming affiliation with "Cyber Anarchists Collective" took responsibility via Telegram, stating the attack was in protest of alleged government surveillance policies. Security researchers noted the group's operational security was weak — the wallet address received only 0.03 BTC (~KSh 250K) in small donations rather than the full ransom, suggesting the attack was more about political messaging than financial gain.

Impact: The president.go.ke domain remained offline for 8 hours while forensic teams completed their investigation and restored the site from clean backups. No classified or citizen data was compromised. However, the incident triggered a government-wide cybersecurity review, including mandatory WAF deployment across all .go.ke domains, accelerated adoption of Certificate Transparency logging, and the formation of a Rapid Response Team (RRT) within KE-CIRT for future incidents.

Key lesson: Government websites are prime targets for both financially motivated attackers and hacktivists. Timely detection, a well-rehearsed incident response plan, and clean offline backups are the difference between a contained incident and a national crisis. Organizations should deploy Web Application Firewalls (WAF) with real-time threat intelligence feeds, enforce hardware MFA for all administrative accounts, conduct monthly tabletop exercises for breach response, and participate in threat intelligence sharing programs like the National KE-CIRT notification system.

SIM Swap connection: This attack followed a pattern seen in earlier Kenyan cyber incidents — attackers often combine phishing (like the Safaricom SIM swap cases in July 2025) with website defacement to maximize disruption. The initial access vector for the president.go.ke breach is still under investigation, but early indicators point to credential harvesting via targeted spear-phishing emails aimed at web administration staff.

Attack vector: Attackers breached a public records aggregator, obtaining a cached database of personal identity records.

KE-CIRT / NCERT Phishing Campaigns

calendar_today 2024 corporate_fare Government Targeted

Phishing campaigns impersonating Kenya's national cyber response center, targeting government employees with fake security alerts.

Attack vector: Spear-phishing emails spoofing KE-CIRT domain, containing malicious attachments disguised as security advisories.

Key lesson: Even security agencies can be impersonated. Verify unexpected alerts through official channels, not the links in the message.

SolarWinds Attack

calendar_today 2020 corporate_fare 18,000+ orgs

Russian state actors compromised SolarWinds' Orion software update, infiltrating US government agencies and Fortune 500 companies.

Attack vector: Backdoor injected into legitimate software update. Every organization that installed the update was compromised.

Key lesson: Verify software integrity. Supply chain attacks bypass all perimeter defenses by trusting legitimate tools.

Ransomware

WannaCry

calendar_today 2017 devices 200K+ computers

Global ransomware attack that crippled hospitals, banks, and governments across 150 countries in hours.

Attack vector: Exploited EternalBlue (CVE-2017-0144), a leaked NSA exploit targeting Windows SMB protocol. Spread laterally without user interaction.

Key lesson: Keep systems updated. Microsoft released a patch months before — organizations that hadn't updated were devastated.

Yahoo Breach

calendar_today 2013–2014 groups 3B accounts

The largest breach in history. Names, emails, passwords, and security Q&A stolen from all 3 billion Yahoo accounts.

Attack vector: State-sponsored actors used credential stuffing and forged cookies to bypass authentication.

Key lesson: Enable MFA everywhere. Passwords alone are never enough — even for "just an email" account.

Phishing / Scam Kenya

M-Pesa Fraud Scams

calendar_today Ongoing warning Thousands of victims

Fraudsters impersonate Safaricom support via calls and SMS, tricking users into sharing M-Pesa PINs or sending money to "reverse" a fake transaction.

Attack vector: Vishing (voice phishing) and smishing. Attackers spoof Safaricom numbers and create urgency ("Your M-Pesa will be suspended").

Key lesson: Safaricom will NEVER ask for your M-Pesa PIN. Hang up and call 100 from your Safaricom line to verify any request.

Safaricom Impersonation Scams

calendar_today Ongoing groups Widespread

Scammers call claiming to be Safaricom "technical support", asking users to install remote access apps or share OTP codes to "fix network issues."

Attack vector: Social engineering via phone calls. Attackers pressure victims with fake urgency and authority.

Key lesson: Legitimate companies never ask you to install remote access software or share OTPs. Always call back on the official number.

Account Takeover Kenya

SIM Swap Fraud

calendar_today Ongoing smartphone M-Pesa Accounts

Attackers convince mobile carriers to reassign a victim's phone number to a new SIM, hijacking M-Pesa and OTP-based authentication.

Attack vector: Social engineering of carrier staff using stolen personal information (ID copies, utility bills).

Key lesson: Use app-based authenticators instead of SMS for 2FA. Report lost signal immediately to your carrier.

WhatsApp Job Scams

calendar_today Ongoing groups Job Seekers

Fraudsters send WhatsApp messages offering "easy money" remote jobs, requiring victims to pay an upfront "registration fee" via M-Pesa.
